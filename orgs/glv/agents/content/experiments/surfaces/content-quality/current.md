# Content Quality — Experiment 7: Surface Rules Applied to FAQ/Service Page Format

## Current Approach

Write all content drafts following the GLV brand voice rules from blog-config.md, with two deliberate additions carried forward from prior experiments:

**Hook engineering rule: open every draft with a single verifiable Northern Ontario-specific fact, then the reader's world, then the product.**

The lede sentence must be:
1. A single, concrete fact drawn from Northern Ontario geography, weather, water systems, or seasonal events — NOT general industry statistics or national averages
2. Followed by the reader identity or seasonal context established in Experiment 2
3. Only then: introduce the product

**What counts as Northern Ontario-specific:**
- St. Marys River, Lake Superior, Batchawana Bay, Georgian Bay, Algoma district specifics
- Sault Ste. Marie weather/snowfall/temperature data
- Northern Ontario season dates (ice-out, walleye opener, freeze-up)
- Algoma district geographic scale or landscape
- Great Lakes water temperature, depth, or behaviour

**What does NOT count (do not use as lede):**
- General industry maintenance intervals ("manufacturers recommend oil change every 50 hours")
- National average statistics not tied to Northern Ontario
- Generic product failure rates or repair cost ranges

**Examples of acceptable lede facts:**
- "Sault Ste. Marie averages 290 cm of snow a year — more than Toronto, Ottawa, and Kingston combined."
- "The St. Marys River drops 6.4 metres in elevation over 1.5 km through the Sault."
- "Ice goes out on Lake Superior in early May most years — two to three weeks later than the inland lakes of the Algoma district."
- "The Algoma district covers 49,000 square kilometres — roughly the size of Nova Scotia."
- "Lake Superior's average depth is 149 metres — its surface stays cold enough to cause cold water shock even in July."
- "Walleye season on most inland lakes in the Algoma district opens on the third Saturday in May."

**Vary the lede structure** across a batch — do not repeat the same sentence pattern. Rotate between: measurement/scale, seasonal date/event, geographic comparison, weather/climate fact.

**Constraint:** Every lede fact must be verifiable from public sources (Stats Canada, Environment Canada, natural geography, Ontario fishing regulations). No invented statistics.

**After the lede:** apply the identity/seasonal-first rule from Experiment 2 as the second sentence before naming the product. That rule is now baseline — keep it.

**Only after this opening** does the draft name or describe the product.

**Body structure rule (Experiment 5 addition):** After the lede, structure the body using the CMI 4-stage outline:

1. **Reader pain** — name the specific problem or seasonal pressure the reader is facing (one short paragraph, no product yet)
2. **Product as solution** — introduce the product/service as the answer, with one concrete differentiator (why this product, why Reyco)
3. **Proof via specifics** — at least one of: named expert quote, real service timeline, specific part number or brand, verifiable stat, or customer outcome (no vague claims)
4. **Objection answer + CTA** — before the closing CTA, address the most common buyer hesitation for this topic with a real number, timeline, or credential (e.g. "Most outboard service appointments take 2-3 days — book before the opener and we'll have you back on the water in time." Not: "Our team is ready to help.")

**Vary the proof type** across a batch — rotate between: named expert insight, service timeline, product spec, seasonal deadline.

All other brand voice rules remain unchanged:
- Educational, direct, professional but approachable
- Canadian English throughout
- No banned phrases or AI clichés
- Short sentences, short paragraphs
- Local Northern Ontario context always included
- Clear CTA at the end of every piece
- Named expert used for credibility where relevant (use confirmed staff roster — Casey, Aaron, Lee, Damian, Lynn, Ron, Cody)
- No invented data — all facts verifiable

For social posts: personal brand voice as Aiden (founder), showing real agency work and AI tools in action.

**FAQ/Service page format adaptation (Experiment 7 addition):** When applying these rules to FAQ pages or service pages (not long-form blog posts), adapt the CMI structure as follows:

- **Page opening**: Lead with a NO-specific fact in the intro paragraph, then establish who this page is for (identity-first). Keep this brief — 2-3 sentences.
- **FAQ answer structure** (per question): Direct answer first (1 sentence) → brief context/pain acknowledgment → specific proof (named staff, service timeline, local detail) → next step or related CTA. Each answer: 3-6 sentences max.
- **Proof required in every answer without exception** (Experiment 8 addition): Every FAQ answer — including logistics answers (hours, location, shipping, financing) — must contain at least one of: (a) named staff member, (b) a real number or timeline, or (c) a specific local/operational constraint. "Redirect only" answers (e.g., "call us for details") are not acceptable as standalone closers. If a logistics answer has no natural staff name or timeline, anchor it with an operational constraint ("we focus on in-person service because…") or a local detail ("SSM is a drive-in market — most customers pick up same-week").
- **Section groupings**: Organise questions by buyer intent stage — general/brand questions first, then service/process questions, then logistics (hours, location, pricing). This mirrors the CMI reader journey at the page level.
- **Lede fact selection for FAQ/service pages**: Choose a fact that speaks to the breadth or depth of the local service relationship — season length, geographic scope, or a fleet/inventory-scale fact. Not a product-specific fact (those belong in product pages).
